The Observation
Circle Division by 1/n
The unit circle is naturally divided by the lines at angles 2π/n for each integer n. These are the positions of r = 1 on ring n — the universal survivor. Between consecutive 1/n lines, each sector contains a specific set of coprime residues.
Mirror Symmetry
For every ring M, R(M) is symmetric under r ↔ M−r:
gcd(M−r, M) = gcd(r, M)
So the TOP half (r/M < ½) and BOTTOM half (r/M > ½) are exact mirrors. Every coprime point in the top has a partner in the bottom.
Prime p Crosses the Equator
Prime p's spiral traces angle p/M as M increases.
p/M = ½ when M = 2p
But at M=2p: gcd(p, 2p) = p > 1 — p is not on ring 2p. The crossing happens in a gap. The spiral jumps the equator invisibly.
■ M < 2p: p in TOP half
■ M = 2p: GAP (p blocked)
■ M > 2p: p in BOTTOM half
The Mirror is Always Valid
When p is in the TOP (M < 2p), its mirror M−p is in the BOTTOM.
gcd(M−p, M) = gcd(p, M) = 1
since p is prime and M < 2p means p∤M. So M−p is always coprime to M — it always exists on the ring as a valid point. The mirror path traces the bottom half perfectly.
The Left-Side Inward Pattern
The two paths approaching the equator from the left (θ near π):

(M+1)/2 path — upper approach, angle just above 1/2.
gcd((M+1)/2, M+1) = (M+1)/2 ≠ 1
This path never lifts. Every point is blocked because r = (M+1)/2 always divides M+1.

(M-1)/2 path — lower approach, angle just below 1/2.
gcd((M-1)/2, M+1) = gcd((M-1)/2, 2)
Lifts iff (M-1)/2 is odd, i.e. M ≡ 3 (mod 4). Perfect alternation: lift, block, lift, block…
What the Left Side Encodes
The (M+1)/2 path is the permanent gap boundary — the frontier that no lift can cross coming inward from the left. It marks where the 1/2 constraint becomes absolute.

The alternating (M-1)/2 path encodes the parity of M: whether M ≡ 1 or 3 (mod 4) determines lift survival. This is related to the Gaussian integers — primes that split vs remain inert in Z[i].
